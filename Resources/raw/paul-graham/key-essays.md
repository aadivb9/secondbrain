## Do Things That Don't Scale

**Core thesis:** Successful startups require founders to perform unscalable, labor-intensive tasks initially. Like a car engine needing manual cranking before running.

**Manual user recruitment** — most critical unscalable activity:
- Founders resist due to shyness/laziness, prefer coding
- "At least one founder (usually the CEO) will spend a lot of time on sales and marketing"
- **Collison installation** (Stripe): "give me your laptop" — set up immediately rather than sending links

**Compound growth math:**
- 100 users + 10% weekly growth = 14,000 after year 1, 2M after year 2
- Compounds despite appearing insignificant initially

**Initial fragility:**
- Nearly all early-stage startups extremely vulnerable
- Airbnb survived via "30 days of going out and engaging in person with users"

**Customer delight:**
- Wufoo sent handwritten thank-you notes to every new user
- Builds loyalty + advocacy + invaluable feedback + permanent culture

**Focused markets:**
- Facebook → only Harvard students initially. "Contained fire" strategy
- Concentrated critical mass before expanding

**Hardware ("pulling a Meraki"):**
- Pebble manufactured hundreds of watches themselves before Kickstarter
- Reveals design insights impossible otherwise

**Consulting/B2B approach:**
- Treat single customer as consulting engagement, perfect, then discover adjacent markets
- Viaweb built online stores for merchants personally → learned merchant perspectives

**Automation timing:**
- Can fake automation manually behind the scenes (Stripe initially)
- Enables faster launches while learning what should be automated

**Launch myth debunked:**
- No one remembers successful startup launches
- What matters is user satisfaction after months of operation

**Startup ideas as vectors:** product + unscalable initial effort

## Startups in 13 Sentences

1. **Pick good cofounders** — "for a startup what location is for real estate"
2. **Launch fast** — reveals what to actually build
3. **Let your idea evolve** — most valuable ideas emerge during implementation
4. **Understand your users** — value = users × improvement in their lives
5. **Make a few users love you** — "easier to expand userwise than satisfactionwise"
6. **Offer surprisingly good customer service** — exceed expectations to learn
7. **You make what you measure** — "put a big piece of paper on your wall"
8. **Spend little** — enables iteration, prevents top failure mode
9. **Get ramen profitable** — changes investor dynamics and morale
10. **Avoid distractions** — "nothing kills startups like distractions"
11. **Don't get demoralized** — distractions cause failure more than money
12. **Don't give up** — "sheer effort is usually enough, so long as you keep morphing your idea"
13. **Deals fall through** — treat agreements as background processes; ignore until done

## Schlep Blindness

**Core concept:** Unconscious tendency to avoid ideas requiring tedious unpleasant work. "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps."

**Key argument:** "A company is defined by the schleps it will undertake." Aversion operates subconsciously, preventing recognition of viable opportunities.

**Stripe example:**
- Payment processing was tedious for 10+ years (banks, fraud, regulations, security)
- Thousands of devs built recipe sites instead — schleps too intimidating
- Stripe's founders chose the schlep → massive opportunity

**Two practical insights:**
1. **Ignorance as advantage**: young founders underestimate both growth potential AND obstacles — offsetting mistakes. Experienced founders see only difficulties.
2. **Reframe the question**: instead of "what should I solve?" ask "what problem do I wish someone else would solve for me?" — bypasses schlep blindness

## Founder Mode

**Two modes:**
- **Founder mode** — deep involvement across organization
- **Manager mode** — modular: CEO directs reports, no detail involvement. "Hire good people and give them room to do their jobs"

**The manager-mode problem:** Frequently enables underperformers to harm companies. Conventional wisdom about scaling = mistaken.

**Brian Chesky example:**
- Followed conventional advice as Airbnb grew → disastrous results
- Recovered by studying Steve Jobs' approach instead
- Multiple founders confirm identical failures with manager mode

**Why founder mode works:** Founders possess capabilities managers lack. Manager mode feels broken to founders because it actually is.

**Path forward:** Likely abandon constraint limiting CEO engagement to direct reports only. Jobs-style annual retreats for crucial personnel — selected by impact, not hierarchy.
