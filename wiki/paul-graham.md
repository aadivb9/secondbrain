## Who He Is

Co-founder of Y Combinator (2005). Lisp hacker (Viaweb → Yahoo Store). Essayist whose ~200 essays at paulgraham.com are the canonical operating manual for startup founders. Mentor to Altman, Tan, Chesky, Collison brothers, etc.

## The Greatest Hits — Five Foundational Ideas

### 1. Do Things That Don't Scale

**Core thesis:** Startups need founders to perform unscalable, manual, labor-intensive work first. Like a car engine that needs cranking before it runs.

| Tactic | Example |
|--------|---------|
| **Manual user recruitment** | Stripe's "Collison installation" — "give me your laptop" |
| **30 days in person** | Airbnb survival mode |
| **Wow each user** | Wufoo's handwritten thank-you notes |
| **Contained fire** | Facebook starting at Harvard only |
| **Pulling a Meraki** | Pebble manually assembling watches |
| **Single-customer consulting** | Viaweb building each merchant's store personally |
| **Manual-as-software** | Stripe faking automation behind the scenes |

**Math of compounding:** 100 users + 10% weekly = 14,000 in year 1, 2M in year 2.

**Launch myth debunked:** No one remembers successful launches. What matters is user satisfaction after months.

### 2. Startups in 13 Sentences

The most condensed startup advice ever written:

1. Pick good cofounders — "for a startup what location is for real estate"
2. Launch fast — reveals what to actually build
3. Let your idea evolve — most valuable ideas emerge during implementation
4. Understand your users — value = users × improvement in lives
5. Make a few users love you — "easier to expand userwise than satisfactionwise"
6. Offer surprisingly good service
7. You make what you measure — "put a big piece of paper on your wall"
8. Spend little
9. Get ramen profitable
10. Avoid distractions — "nothing kills startups like distractions"
11. Don't get demoralized
12. Don't give up — "sheer effort is usually enough, so long as you keep morphing"
13. Deals fall through — treat as background processes; ignore until done

### 3. Schlep Blindness

**Core concept:** Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps.

> "A company is defined by the schleps it will undertake."

**Stripe example:** Payment processing was tedious for 10+ years (banks, fraud, regulations, security). Thousands of devs built recipe sites instead. Stripe chose the schlep → massive opportunity.

**Reframe:** Instead of "what should I solve?" ask **"what problem do I wish someone else would solve for me?"** This bypasses the unconscious filter.

---

### 4. Founder Mode

**Two modes:**
- **Founder mode** — deep involvement across organization
- **Manager mode** — modular, "hire good people and give them room"

The manager-mode default fails for founders. Brian Chesky learned this the hard way — recovered by studying Steve Jobs.

**Why:** Founders possess capabilities managers lack. Manager mode feels broken to founders because it actually is.

**Practice:** Jobs-style annual retreats for crucial personnel — selected by impact, not hierarchy.

---

### 5. Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule (canonical, not re-extracted)

- **Maker's schedule:** big blocks of uninterrupted time. A single meeting can destroy a half-day.
- **Manager's schedule:** cut into 1-hour intervals.
- Don't impose manager schedule on makers.

## Implementable for Aadi

- **Schlep blindness check** — for any idea, ask: what schlep am I unconsciously avoiding? That's where the opportunity is.
- **Maker schedule discipline** — protect long blocks for hard work; batch admin/comms
- **Big paper on the wall** — pick one metric for lockedIn (DAU? quiz pass rate?) and post it visibly
- **Live in the future** — you're already in the AI-native generation. What's obvious to you that adults don't see?
- **Don't give up + morph the idea** — the project that succeeds will look different from the one you start
