## Admissions Strategy (Liz's Doctrine)

### Pointy vs. Well-Rounded
- **Well-rounded:** Strong across subjects, clear major, sustained involvement. Lower risk. Works at most schools.
- **Pointy:** Dominant in one field, national/international validation. Higher risk — only works if spike is undeniable.
- **Key insight:** Well-rounded students CAN get into pointy-preferring schools (MIT, Stanford, Caltech) if they prove they're "the best" in their field via external data: competitions, research, publications.
- Schools that explicitly value pointy: MIT, Stanford, UPenn CE specifically.

### What Admissions Officers Actually Look For
1. **Long-term commitment** (9-12) in 8-10 activities — depth over breadth
2. **External validation** — AOs are NOT subject experts; they need outside proof (competition results, publications, faculty endorsements)
3. **KPIs** — rankings, percentiles, quantified growth, concrete outputs
4. **3-5 activities tied to intended major** — shows academic center of gravity
5. **Measurable community impact** — student-identified problems, student-led solutions
6. **Surprise factor** — unusual combos, independent projects, authenticity

### Activity List Rules
- Max 10 activities on Common App (up to 20 on UC)
- 150 chars for description — start with action verb, quantify everything
- Never use "I", "we", "our" — direct, impersonal, punchy
- Include technical terms from your field (shows depth)
- Prioritize: leadership + scale + originality + impact
- **Strong activity description formula:** [Action verb] + [what you did] + [measurable result]

**Good examples from PathIvy:**
> "Taught 500+ beginner to advanced Black Belt classes with 7-20 students ages 5-60 over 4 years. Specialized in head-height & spinning jump kicks"
> "Increased membership by 20%. Mentored 500+ members on business research papers, SWOT analysis, profit/loss ratios, Ansoff Matrix."

### Personal Statement Rules (from Liz)
- Share **life experiences**, not activities or beliefs
- Be **specific** — avoid "our universe" type language (reads as insulting/presumptuous)
- Max ~650 words — 646 words was flagged as too long
- Avoid: summary of activities, generic "I want to help the world"
- **Caltech** wants: highly specialized STEM genius, extreme focus, speaks at international conferences level
- **Harvard** wants: well-rounded but with clear domain excellence + broad abilities + empathy/emotional growth
- **CMU/JHU** want: deep passion in niche areas, intellectual curiosity
- **UPenn** wants: community impact using skills locally, not external savior narrative
- Balance personal statement with your major — don't write a CS essay if applying to schools with "Why major" supplements

### Essay Rubric (PathIvy)
| Dimension | Exemplary |
|---|---|
| Ideas & Content | Deeply/originally engages prompt; nuanced, compelling |
| Structure | Clear logical progression; smooth transitions; strong intro/conclusion |
| Voice & Tone | Distinctive consistent personal voice; appropriate and memorable |
| Mechanics | Virtually error-free; varied/complex sentence structure |

- Can't use race as a checkbox factor
- CAN tell stories about how identity shaped experiences — through specific narrative

### Institutional Priorities Reality Check
Colleges aren't just picking best students — they're curating a class. They consider:
- Geographic diversity
- First-gen status
- Athletic recruitment
- Legacy/donor relationships
- Gender balance in majors
- Financial need/ability to pay
- Demonstrated interest

Indian/Chinese families typically lack legacy connections → must compensate with uniqueness + external validation.

## Key Admissions Insights (Liz's Doctrine, Synthesized)

1. **Depth > breadth.** 8-10 deep activities beat 30 shallow ones every time.
2. **External proof is everything.** AOs can't judge if your code is good — they need a competition result, publication, or faculty letter.
3. **Quantify everything.** "Led club" < "Grew club from 12 to 80 members, won regional championship."
4. **App theme = narrative thread.** Every activity, essay, and recommendation should reinforce the same story.
5. **Early Decision gives wealthy students an unfair advantage** — Aadi should be strategic about which schools to ED.
6. **US News rankings are misleading** for undergrads — they measure faculty reputation, not teaching quality. Fit > rank.
7. **"Well-rounded" can beat "pointy"** if you can prove you're the best in your field with data.
8. **Indian CE/CS applicant in Seattle** = high-competition demographic. Must differentiate with defying interests + unique narrative.
9. **Local community impact > international savior narrative** (e.g., UPenn specifically dislikes external savior framing).
10. **Personal statement = personality, not resume recap.** Show who you are, not what you did.
